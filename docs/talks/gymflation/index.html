Premise

Social media is distorting the perceived average strength level.

Much like it is distorting beauty standards, success standards, relationship standards, politics, etc. Quicker to list fields that haven’t been distorted by social media.

What is gymflation?

  • An excuse to show you some LLM techniques.
  • A phenomenon connected to social media’s negative effects on self-image, which is increasingly leading to mental health issues such as body dysmorphia.
  • Mostly the former though.

One of the fascinating things over the last three years has been how traditional machine learning techniques have evolved and become accessible to people who wouldn’t normally consider natural language processing and computer vision to be tools they can call on. Although I’m personally rather interested in online gym culture - which I hasten to add isn’t entirely bad, there is some positive information and motivation sharing - in this talk it’s more of a platform to let us explore how you can make good use of LLMs in a research project.

Comparison to superhero inflation

“When I grow up, I want to be as strong as Batman” is less achievable now than it was in the 60s.

Body dysmorphia and the ill-effects of social media are real and growing problems, but I don’t want to overstate my credibility in that area - I’m not a social scientist, just an interested amateur.

However it doesn’t strike me as controversial to say that some cultural standards have changed rapidly in recent times.

Plan

How can we investigate this?

Measure if feats of strength reported in online videos are increasing.

Taking a narrow definition of strength: deadlift personal record (PR) videos on YouTube.

Questions:

  1. Is the average reported deadlift PR weight increasing over time?
  2. Are the number of reported heavy deadlift PRs increasing over time?
  • Deadlifting is the method that lets one lift the most weight, so it’s a point of pride for many lifters.
  • It goes like this - the bar starts on the floor, you bend over, and you straighten up to lift the bar to hip height. A lot of muscles get involved in this, primarily your erector spinae (the long back muscles on either side of your spine) and the glutes (buttocks), and also your quads (thighs). It’s important to keep your back straight to avoid injury.
  • Typically people will report the maximum weight that they can lift for one repetition. That’s known as your One Rep Max, or 1RM. Your best ever is your personal record, or PR. A rule of thumb is that after a few years of serious training, someone would be able to deadlift over twice their bodyweight.
  • I say “questions” rather than “hypotheses” because we’re not doing science here, we’re playing.

Steps

  • Scrape the YouTube API for 10 years of deadlift personal record (PR) videos with httr2
  • Use structured responses to extract the lifted weight with an LLM
  • Evaluate the quality of our extraction and improve it with In-Context Learning (ICL)
  • Use a multimodal model to judge the lifter’s gender
  • Make extensive use of caching so errors don’t set us back
  • Plot our results with good old ggplot2

Instagram, TikTok and YouTube are all popular among the lifting community (and probably many more) but YouTube is relatively open with its data, making it our best choice.

Data

Regular expressions wouldn’t be sufficient here…

A reasonable question is whether we could use regular expressions, regexes, to extract the weights. There is often a pattern after all. However, in practice it’s clear that people are mixing in their bodyweight and age, so it would be pretty error prone. Luckily in 2026 we have a new option.

Model 1: fact extraction

Using an LLM to extract the weights from the messy text.

Once upon a time, one would train either a sequence-2-sequence model or a small generative transformer, but now the great big NLP box in the sky provides. We no longer need to fire up Python, or even have a GPU.

At this point it’s good to check familiarity. Is everyone aware that LLMs are not only chatbots, but can be used programmatically via HTTP APIs? In dead simple terms, you can send a message to OpenAI’s servers, and they will run ChatGPT on it and send the answer back. OpenAI isn’t the only AI lab in town of course, and in the examples that follow I’m mostly going to use an open source (free) model that’s small enough to run on my laptop.

We’re going to use the R library Ellmer. We will make use of structured responses to ensure that we don’t have to parse the response text ourselves, it’s guaranteed to be formatted in the JSON object of our choice.

Structured responses

That local caching proxy isn’t a technological marvel: it checks if I’ve made a request with the same model and inputs before. If not, it sends it off to the remote server and saves the response to disk, wheras if I have it will just copy back the saved response from last time. This saves time/money/energy.

The model is from the French lab Mistral, the only major AI lab with an HQ in Europe unless you count Google DeepMind. They make their smaller models available for free and if you have enough of a GPU on your computer you can run it yourself. That’s nice because you’re not paying anything except your electricity bill, and you get complete privacy. It also lets tinkerers play with the innards of a model and do cool stuff: we can thank open source models for a lot of innovations.

Structured generation is dead simple conceptually: at each generation step, instead of sampling from all possible tokens, sample only from tokens that are legal according to your schema. Theoretically any grammar is fine, but in practice everyone is using JSON schema.

If we only prompted the LLM to return JSON in our desired format, but didn’t restriIct its outputs, it would mostly return the right format. Structured generation makes this reliable. It’s usually best to keep the format instructions in the prompt though, so we get sensible probabilities on the legal tokens.

Evaluating our model

  • AI is jagged intelligence: screws up in unexpected ways
  • Need to measure its performance
  • Build high-quality test set and score accuracy (dev + test) 1
  • This model so far: 73% accurate in its extractions
  • Let’s open the model optimisation toolbox!

The most important thing is to evaluate the model on your task. Since in 2026 your “model” is a few lines of prompt text, the evaluation is the most valuable asset you have. And indeed, exciting systems exist that will generate optimal prompt text for you, so please don’t treasure a few lines of hand-crafted prompt. The evaluation dataset is the gold.

One of the techniques I considered showing you was getting a very large model to annotate your test set. But it’s TURTLES ALL THE WAY DOWN, because you need to be confident in the large model’s accuracy first.

A quick audience check, are you familiar with the concept of a dev set and a held out test set? This is worth knowing if you intend to try and optimise a model. The short version is that you split your dataset into two parts: a dev(elopment) set and a test set. Do initial assessments and iteration on the dev set, but keep the test set out of sight until you want to stop and score your final model. That way you avoid overfitting, in other words you avoid making optimisations that don’t generalise.

In-Context Learning (ICL)

extract_dl_weight_model <- function(turns = list()) {
  chat <- chat_openai_compatible(
    LLM_SERVER_URL,
    model = "mistralai/ministral-3-3b",
    credentials = function() "", # not used - local model
    params = params(max_tokens = 50), # don't generate more than we need
    system_prompt = system.prompt
  )
  chat$set_turns(turns)
  chat
}

example_turn_pair <- function(title, description, weight, unit, reps) {
  list(
    UserTurn(list(ContentText(
      interpolate("Title: {{title}}\nDescription: {{description}}")
    ))),
    AssistantTurn(list(ContentText(
      interpolate(
        "{\"weight\":{{weight}}, \"unit\":\"{{unit}}\", \"reps\":{{reps}}}"
      )
    )))
  )
}

A.k.a. “few-shot learning”, the idea is you condition the LLM on example inputs and outputs.

Here I’ve hand-crafted some examples based on dev failures.

New result: 92% extraction accuracy on test.

By the way, with current reasoning models ICL is sometimes detrimental because the LLM can over-condition on the examples at the expense of doing additional reasoning of its own. For this reason it’s always necessary to evaluate.

Model 2: image classification

Male and female lifters have different strength distributions. Changes in one could mask changes in the other.

This is a big and obvious potential confounder, so we should investigate further.

For what it’s worth I also took at a look at different lifting styles. The choices are conventional and sumo, the latter being where you spread your legs wider than your arms so your back is more upright and your legs do more of the work. Debate rages on in the lifting community, but I didn’t find any difference.

Evaluating our image model

  • Another cycle of dataset annotation, evaluation and optimisation.
  • This time I’ve vibe-coded an annotation UI.
  • Though the code for this UI is absolutely disposable, it’s absolutely worth your time designing a UI that minimises cognitive load, is efficient to manipulate, and doesn’t lead to mistakes.
  • There’s really no substitute for getting to know your own data by the way. It’s absolutely worth your time. Unless you were intimately involved in collecting the data, it will surprise you. And more than that, I feel it’s only when you get into the data that insights arrive. For example my naive version of this image model was providing only the image - isn’t that enough? Well it turned out that often the image was unclear but the channel name or the title was enough to disambiguate. I also found that some of the images weren’t actually real people, they were anime people, so I had to steer the model around that.
  • I should mention that an improvement on my approach here would be to have multiple judges, check their agreement, and perhaps have multiple iterations of refining the coding guidelines and labelling the data.
